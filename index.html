Case Overview

Torrence Holt Becker, a Manchester resident and custodial parent, filed a seven-count federal civil rights complaint documenting two connected crises: a coordinated October 2024 scheme in which city welfare, code enforcement, and DCYF allegedly worked in concert to circumvent his Fourth Amendment rights — leaving his children camping in 37°F rain — and a warrantless April 2025 police welfare check that ended with a systematic visual sweep of his home and a falsified incident report. After preliminary screening, six counts were dismissed and one survived: Count IV, a Fourth Amendment warrantless search claim against Officers McCarthy, Battye, and the City of Manchester. The dismissed counts — and the documented conduct behind them — remain part of the public record.

SURVIVING AFTER PRELIMINARY SCREENING
Count IV
Fourth Amendment — Warrantless Search

Magistrate Judge Saint-Marc found that Becker states a viable Fourth Amendment claim against Officer Battye (warrantless entry into the apartment living room) and Officer McCarthy (entry into the building stairwell, whose private status requires factual development). The claim against the City of Manchester also survives under Monell municipal liability. All other defendants on Count IV — Dionne, Moran, Michaud, and Mark — were dismissed. District Judge McCafferty approved this recommendation in full on November 6, 2025.

On October 7, 2024, Torrence Becker exercised his constitutional right to deny DCYF entry to his home. Within hours, a DCYF worker called Manchester Code Enforcement and asked them to treat the Fourth Amendment assertion as a formal "complaint" — so an inspector could gain entry instead. At the same time, the Manchester Welfare Department refused to provide emergency housing assistance, telling Becker that child safety concerns were DCYF's problem. DCYF then went unreachable for 72+ hours. The result: Becker's children, including one with an active respiratory infection, were forced to camp in a Weare campground in 37°F weather with no adequate gear.

Six months later, on April 3, 2025, Manchester police Officers McCarthy and Battye conducted a welfare check at Becker's apartment. According to Becker's allegations, Battye entered the living room without a warrant and conducted a systematic visual sweep of multiple rooms — including a child's bedroom — while McCarthy's official incident report recorded the encounter as ending at 3:00 PM when it actually ran until 3:45 PM. A federal civil rights complaint followed. After preliminary review, the Fourth Amendment warrantless search claim against Battye, McCarthy, and the City survived and has been ordered to service. The conduct behind the dismissed counts — documented by signed admissions, coordination logs, and official correspondence — remains part of the public record.

The Architecture of Unaccountability

Becker's complaint describes not one constitutional violation, but a self-reinforcing system — two interlocking schemes, each designed so that every door a citizen might open for help closes immediately behind them.

In October 2024, when Becker exercised his Fourth Amendment right to deny DCYF entry, two things happened simultaneously: DCYF called Code Enforcement to manufacture an administrative workaround, and City Welfare refused emergency housing by deferring back to DCYF — the very agency that had just gone dark. With DCYF unreachable and Welfare refusing to act, there was nowhere left to turn. When Becker tried to escalate — to a supervisor, a director, an alderman — each path closed. Six months later, when police entered his home without a warrant and he filed complaints, the same architecture repeated itself: an internal investigation that exonerated officers while simultaneously documenting the violation as "standard protocol," a civilian review board that deferred to the flawed investigation, and a captain who responded to detailed constitutional appeals with a single sentence: "This matter has been looked into by MPD and the CRC and is closed barring any new information."

Scheme One — Oct. 2024

The DCYF-Welfare-Code Enforcement Triangle. Citizen asserts Fourth Amendment rights against DCYF → DCYF immediately contacts Code Enforcement to use administrative inspection as a proxy → City Welfare simultaneously refuses emergency housing, deferring to DCYF → DCYF goes unreachable → No path to assistance exists that doesn't require surrendering constitutional rights. Outcome: children camping in 37°F rain.

Scheme Two — April 2025

The Welfare Check as Warrantless Search. Officers arrive for welfare check → Officer Battye enters apartment living room without warrant → conducts visual sweep of child's bedroom, kitchen, second living area → McCarthy submits incident report with falsified timestamps → MPD Internal Affairs opens one-day investigation → Officers exonerated → Investigation memo simultaneously admits the conduct was "standard protocol."

The Complaint Architecture

Five Closed Doors. Complaint filed with MPD → IA investigation closes in one day, officers exonerated → CRC review defers to flawed IA findings, finds "insufficient cause" → Captain Gravelle responds to three detailed constitutional appeals: "closed barring any new information" → Chief Marr receives formal escalation: no response. Federal lawsuit filed four days later.

If Every Door Closes, Is That Policy?

The exhibits document a supervisor signing a statement refusing to hear child safety concerns, a DCYF contact log showing same-day coordination to circumvent Fourth Amendment rights, an investigation memo admitting warrantless home searches are "standard protocol," and a captain who responded to three substantive constitutional appeals with two sentences — identical both times. Every formal channel Becker used produced either silence or a form response.

Under Monell v. Dep't of Social Services, a municipality is liable under § 1983 when a constitutional violation results from official policy, a widespread custom, or deliberate indifference by final policymakers to known constitutional violations. The question in this case is whether what is documented here represents isolated error — or a system that functioned exactly as designed.

Legal Arguments

Plaintiff's Position

Officer Battye — Warrantless Entry and Search, No Exception Applies

The Fourth Amendment's protection of the home is at its apex. Becker alleges Battye entered his apartment living room without a warrant, without his consent, and without exigent circumstances — after the purpose of the welfare check had already been fulfilled and his child's safety confirmed. The welfare check exception does not permit officers to remain inside a private residence conducting visual sweeps of bedrooms, kitchens, and secondary rooms. Becker further argues that MPD's own internal investigation memo confirms the violation: Whelan's memo admits Battye "stepped just inside of the threshold," "looks left and right," and "briefly leans toward the bedroom" — and justifies it as "standard protocol." An admitted standard practice of warrantless home searches is not an exception to the Fourth Amendment — it is a Monell violation.

This is the surviving claim. The Magistrate found it plausible on its face at preliminary review. Battye will need to establish a recognized exception (exigent circumstances, consent, or community caretaking) that applies after welfare is confirmed — a difficult factual and legal argument when the government's own memo admits the conduct was routine rather than circumstance-driven.

Officer McCarthy — Private Stairwell Carries Fourth Amendment Protection

Becker alleges McCarthy entered a stairwell that is exclusively private: it bears only Becker's address (162), contains his mail slot and personal mail, displays his children's artwork and named trophies, holds a family wedding book labeled "BECKER," and leads to only one door. The Magistrate acknowledged the stairwell's private status "requires additional consideration" but found the claim plausible for preliminary review purposes. Becker argues that no reasonable person — and certainly not a trained police officer — could look at that stairwell and conclude it was a common area. The question is whether Becker had a reasonable expectation of privacy there, and he argues the physical contents answer that question conclusively.

City of Manchester — Monell Municipal Liability

A municipality is liable under § 1983 when constitutional violations result from official policy, a widespread custom, or a final policymaker's deliberate indifference to known violations. Becker argues all three are established here. Official policy: Whelan's memo explicitly calls warrantless home searches during welfare checks "standard protocol." Custom: Becker was told by a sergeant on April 3 that Battye's conduct was consistent with department policy. Final policymaker ratification: Chief Marr, with final policymaking authority over MPD, received detailed constitutional escalation and responded with silence. The Magistrate agreed, at preliminary review, that Becker states a plausible Monell claim.

The Dismissed Counts — The Record Still Speaks

Becker's objection argues the Magistrate's dismissal of six counts rests on treating documented municipal policies as isolated incidents. On Count I (substantive due process), Becker contends the conscience-shocking standard is met not by any single act but by the escalating institutional pattern: a welfare worker signs a refusal to hear child safety concerns, a supervisor ratifies it by phone, a director ratifies it in writing, and children wind up camping in freezing rain — all documented step by step. On Count V (conspiracy), Becker argues the DCYF contact log IS the overt act, and the Magistrate's dismissal for "no completed inspection" overlooks that an inspection DID occur the following day. These arguments are preserved for appeal.

Anticipated Defense Position

Community Caretaking / Welfare Check Exception

Officers McCarthy and Battye will likely argue their entry falls within recognized exceptions to the warrant requirement — specifically the "community caretaking" function and the welfare check doctrine. Under this argument, officers conducting a welfare check may enter a residence when they have reasonable grounds to believe someone inside needs assistance. The check was initiated by the child's school following multiple absences, and Becker's child had reportedly been hospitalized previously. Battye's brief visual check of living conditions may be characterized as a reasonable extension of confirming the child's welfare.

Plaintiff's counter: The welfare check exception, even if valid at entry, does not authorize a systematic visual sweep of bedrooms and secondary rooms after welfare is confirmed. The Whelan memo's own admission that this was "standard protocol" — not a response to any observed emergency — fatally undermines the exigency argument. An officer cannot convert a completed welfare check into an investigative search.

Stairwell Is a Common Area — No Reasonable Expectation of Privacy

McCarthy will likely argue that the stairwell constitutes a common hallway in a multi-unit building, where courts have generally held tenants have a diminished expectation of privacy. Under this argument, the stairwell is accessible to the landlord, other residents, and guests — making it more analogous to a shared lobby than a private space. The Magistrate cited United States v. Johnson (4th Cir. 2025) for the proposition that the stairwell's private status requires factual development about the building's configuration and access.

Plaintiff's counter: Exhibit 14 documents that this stairwell bears Becker's exclusive address, contains only his mail, displays only his children's belongings by name, and leads to only one door. These are not facts that require discovery — they are Becker's documented allegations. A stairwell leading exclusively to one unit, displaying that unit's family belongings and address, is functionally indistinguishable from a foyer or entranceway — both treated as private.

Qualified Immunity — No Clearly Established Right Violated

Officers may assert qualified immunity, arguing that even if a Fourth Amendment violation occurred, the law was not "clearly established" that officers conducting welfare checks could not briefly visually assess a home's living conditions, or that the type of stairwell at issue was private. Qualified immunity protects officers from liability unless a reasonable officer would have known the specific conduct violated the Constitution at the time of the act.

Plaintiff's counter: The Fourth Amendment rule against warrantless home entries is among the most clearly established doctrines in constitutional law. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that physical entry into the home "is the chief evil against which the wording of the Fourth Amendment is directed." An officer does not need a case on all fours to know that entering a living room and conducting a visual sweep of bedrooms without a warrant violates the Constitution.

Welfare Department — Reasonable Deference to DCYF

Defendants Moran, Mark, and Michaud — all dismissed — would argue that the Welfare Department's actions were entirely lawful: DCYF was already managing Becker's case, the Welfare Department lacked authority to intervene in DCYF's child safety determinations, and deferring to the agency with statutory authority over child welfare was the legally correct response. The Magistrate agreed, finding no conscience-shocking conduct and no protected interest in emergency welfare assistance on these facts.

Plaintiff's counter (for appeal): The key distinction is timing. On October 7, initial deference may have been reasonable. But on October 10, with safety concerns validated, DCYF unreachable for 72 hours, and a specific camping-in-freezing-rain danger imminent, continued refusal — escalated by signing an official document — is not "reasonable deference." It is deliberate indifference to known, confirmed, imminent danger to children.

Constitutional Framework

IV
Fourth Amendment — Surviving Claim

The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches and seizures. Non-consensual entries into a residence without a warrant are presumptively unreasonable. This is the claim that survived. Becker alleges Officer Battye entered his apartment living room and conducted a warrantless visual search of multiple private rooms. The surviving question is whether any recognized exception — exigent circumstances, consent, or community caretaking — justified entry after welfare was confirmed. A second question — whether McCarthy's entry into the stairwell was protected — turns on whether that space is "private" for Fourth Amendment purposes, a factual issue the court found requires further development.

XIV
Fourteenth Amendment — Dismissed Claims

The Due Process Clause prohibits deprivation of life, liberty, or property without due process. Becker alleged both substantive and procedural violations. The substantive due process claim (Count I) required showing conduct "so egregious as to shock the conscience" — the Magistrate found welfare deferral to DCYF did not meet this bar, and the District Judge agreed. The procedural due process claim (Count II) required a protected interest and its deprivation without process — the Magistrate found no protected interest in emergency welfare assistance on these facts. Both are dismissed, but Becker's objection preserves the argument that the escalating institutional pattern — not any single act — satisfies the conscience-shocking standard.

I
First Amendment — Dismissed w/o Prejudice

The First Amendment protects the right to petition the government for redress of grievances, and — under recent circuit precedent — prohibits government officials from blocking constituents from public social media profiles used for official purposes. Becker raised two distinct First Amendment theories: (1) Levasseur's Facebook blocking and "piss off" response to a formal constitutional petition, and (2) the Welfare Department's alleged systemic barriers to emergency assistance requests. Both were dismissed — the Levasseur claims for improper joinder (without prejudice), the Welfare claims for failure to state a petition-clause violation. The Levasseur claim may be refiled separately.

Controlling & Persuasive Authority

Monell v. Dep't of Social Services, 436 U.S. 658 (1978)

Municipalities are "persons" subject to § 1983 liability when a constitutional violation results from official policy, a widespread custom, or deliberate indifference by final policymakers. Directly governs the City of Manchester's liability on Count IV — the Magistrate found Becker states a plausible Monell claim based on the alleged policy of warrantless welfare-check searches.

McCabe v. Life-Line Ambulance Serv., 77 F.3d 540 (1st Cir. 1996)

Non-consensual entries by government agents into a residence without a warrant are presumptively unreasonable under the Fourth Amendment. Cited by the Magistrate as the controlling First Circuit standard governing the warrantless entry claim against Battye and McCarthy.

County of Sacramento v. Lewis, 523 U.S. 833 (1998)

Executive actions violate substantive due process when they are "truly outrageous, uncivilized, and intolerable" — requiring more than "humdrum legal error." Cited by Becker's objection as the standard met by the escalating pattern of institutional refusal leading to children camping in freezing rain, where the municipality doubled down after confirmed danger.

Plourde v. Lewis, No. 23-cv-00323 (D. Me. 2025)

Addresses the community caretaking and exigent circumstances exceptions to the warrant requirement in the welfare check context. Cited by the Magistrate as governing the exceptions framework applicable to the Battye and McCarthy Fourth Amendment claim.

United States v. Johnson, — F.4th — (4th Cir. Aug. 5, 2025)

Addresses Fourth Amendment privacy expectations in apartment building stairwells and common areas, requiring consideration of building configuration and tenant/visitor access. Cited by the Magistrate as the reason McCarthy's stairwell claim requires factual development rather than dismissal — the case was decided two months before the R&R.

Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009)

Establishes the plausibility pleading standard under Rule 12(b)(6): complaints must contain sufficient factual matter to state a claim plausible on its face. The standard applied (with liberal construction for pro se plaintiffs) throughout the Magistrate's preliminary review — six of seven counts failed it; Count IV against McCarthy, Battye, and the City passed it.

Erickson v. Pardus, 551 U.S. 89 (2007)

Pro se complaints are to be construed liberally. The Magistrate applied this standard throughout the R&R, and it is the reason several borderline claims received the most favorable possible reading before being dismissed — including the final survival of the Battye/McCarthy Fourth Amendment claim.

Knight First Amendment Inst. v. Trump, 928 F.3d 226 (2d Cir. 2019)

Government officials who use social media accounts for official purposes cannot block constituents from engaging with those accounts — the interactive space is a designated public forum. Cited by Becker in his formal cease-and-desist to Levasseur as the controlling precedent for the Facebook-blocking claim. The claim was dismissed on joinder grounds, not on the merits of this doctrine.

When Does a Welfare Check Become an Unconstitutional Search?

Police welfare checks are growing in frequency across the country — and so is the legal ambiguity around them. Officers responding to wellness calls occupy unusual constitutional ground: they arrive with legitimate purpose, no warrant, and often with discretion about how long to stay and what to observe. The question this case will help answer is whether a completed welfare check — a child's safety confirmed — can transform into a warrantless residential search based on an officer's general interest in "viewing living conditions." If MPD's admitted "standard protocol" is constitutional, every welfare check becomes a potential warrantless home inspection. If it is not, departments nationwide conducting identical practices face reconsideration.

Can Agencies Coordinate to Circumvent Constitutional Refusals?

The October 2024 DCYF-Code Enforcement coordination — documented in a government log the day after Becker's Fourth Amendment assertion — raises a question courts have rarely confronted with such clarity: what happens when one government agency uses a different agency's administrative authority as a workaround to access what a citizen has constitutionally refused? The warrant requirement does not prohibit coordination for legitimate purposes. But it does prohibit using administrative processes as pretexts for what would otherwise require judicial authorization. The DCYF coordination log's direct reference to Becker's refusal and the plan to send inspectors — with a contingency for if Becker refuses again — describes exactly that structure. The conspiracy claim was dismissed; the question it poses was not.

The Questions Proceeding to Litigation

  • → The Threshold Question — Once a welfare check confirms a child's safety, what legal authority — if any — permits an officer to remain inside a private home and conduct visual searches of rooms not visible from the doorway?
  • → Standard Protocol as Monell Liability — When a municipality's own internal investigation calls warrantless home searches "standard protocol," has the municipality written its own Monell liability into the record?
  • → The Stairwell Question — Does a residential stairwell serving only one apartment, bearing only that apartment's address, and containing only that family's personal belongings, carry Fourth Amendment protection — and if it does, must a warrant exist before officers enter it?
  • → Report Falsification and Credibility — If McCarthy's incident report contains the impossible timeline Becker alleges, what does that mean for the credibility of every fact in that report — including the characterizations that form the basis of the stairwell defense?
  • → Chain of Command Silence as Ratification — When a Chief of Police receives a detailed escalation documenting his department's written admission of unconstitutional "standard protocol" and does not respond, does that silence constitute final-policymaker ratification under Monell?
The Fourth Amendment's protection of the home is, in the words of the Supreme Court, "the chief evil against which the wording of the Fourth Amendment is directed." If a government department can describe warrantless home searches as routine protocol, investigate itself in a single day, receive detailed constitutional appeals documenting its own admission, respond with form letters, and face no consequence — then the protection is nominal. Becker v. City of Manchester is, at its core, a test of whether that protection has substance.
